HISTORY

The Founding (1861–1865)

Natural scientist William Barton Rogers successfully lobbied the Massachusetts legislature for an Act of Incorporation for a polytechnic institute. Delayed by the American Civil War, the institute's first 15 students were admitted in 1865 in Boston's Back Bay.

Boston to Cambridge (1916)

Facing a massive need for campus expansion, the institute relocated across the Charles River to its current, sprawling campus in Cambridge, Massachusetts.

World War II and Defense Research

Under President Karl T. Compton, MIT transformed into a premier research hub. The university administered the U.S. government's Radiation Laboratory—the nation's leading center for radar research.

The Computing Era

Following the war, MIT became deeply embedded in technological and aerospace breakthroughs. It played a foundational role in the creation of early digital computers, the Apollo missions' guidance and computing systems, and early internet networking

Global Open Access (2001)

Launching MIT OpenCourseWare, the institute became a trailblazer in democratizing education by making the syllabus and materials for nearly all its courses freely accessible online.
